Breast Cancer Information

Comprehensive information about breast cancer types, screening, treatment options, and support resources.

Understanding Breast Cancer

Breast cancer occurs when cells in breast tissue grow uncontrollably. It's the second most common cancer in women, but can also affect men. Early detection through screening significantly improves treatment outcomes.

Key Statistics

  • • 1 in 8 women will develop breast cancer in their lifetime
  • • 5-year survival rate is 99% when caught early
  • • Most common in women over 50
  • • About 2,650 men are diagnosed annually

Types of Breast Cancer

Invasive Ductal Carcinoma (IDC)

Most common type (80%), starts in milk ducts and spreads to nearby tissue

Invasive Lobular Carcinoma (ILC)

Starts in milk-producing glands, harder to detect on mammograms

Triple-Negative Breast Cancer

Lacks hormone receptors, more aggressive but responds well to chemotherapy

HER2-Positive

Overexpresses HER2 protein, treated with targeted therapies

Screening & Early Detection

Regular screening saves lives by detecting breast cancer before symptoms appear.

Mammography

X-ray examination of the breast to detect tumors before they can be felt

Recommended:

  • • Ages 40-49: Discuss with doctor
  • • Ages 50-74: Every 2 years
  • • High risk: Earlier and more frequent

Self-Examination

Monthly self-exams help you become familiar with your breasts

What to look for:

  • • New lumps or thickening
  • • Changes in size or shape
  • • Skin dimpling or puckering
  • • Nipple discharge or inversion

Clinical Exam

Physical examination by a healthcare professional

Frequency:

  • • Ages 20-39: Every 3 years
  • • Ages 40+: Annually
  • • Part of routine physical exam
  • • Includes lymph node check

Treatment Options

Treatment plans are personalized based on cancer type, stage, and individual factors.

Local Treatments

Surgery

Lumpectomy

Removes tumor and small amount of surrounding tissue

Mastectomy

Removes entire breast, may include lymph nodes

Reconstruction

Rebuilds breast shape after mastectomy

Radiation Therapy

  • • Usually follows lumpectomy
  • • May be used after mastectomy
  • • Targets remaining cancer cells
  • • Typically 5-6 weeks of treatment

Systemic Treatments

Chemotherapy

  • • May be given before or after surgery
  • • Destroys cancer cells throughout body
  • • Various drug combinations available
  • • Treatment cycles with rest periods

Hormone Therapy

  • • For hormone receptor-positive cancers
  • • Blocks estrogen or progesterone
  • • Tamoxifen, aromatase inhibitors
  • • Usually 5-10 years of treatment

Targeted Therapy

  • • Targets specific cancer cell features
  • • HER2-targeted drugs (Herceptin)
  • • CDK4/6 inhibitors
  • • Often combined with other treatments
