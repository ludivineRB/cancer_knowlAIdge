Colorectal Cancer Information

Key facts about colorectal cancer, early detection, treatment strategies, and support resources for patients and families.

Understanding Colorectal Cancer

Colorectal cancer starts in the colon or rectum and usually begins as small growths called polyps. Screening and early removal of polyps can prevent cancer development.

Key Statistics

  • • 3rd most common cancer worldwide
  • • Affects both men and women equally
  • • Risk increases after age 50
  • • Highly preventable through screening

Types of Colorectal Cancer

Adenocarcinomas

Most common type (95%), arising from glandular cells in the lining of the colon and rectum

Carcinoid Tumors

Arises from hormone-producing cells of the intestine

Other Rare Types

Includes gastrointestinal stromal tumors (GISTs), lymphomas, and squamous cell carcinomas

Screening & Early Detection

Regular screening can detect polyps and early-stage cancer, improving outcomes significantly.

Colonoscopy

Gold standard for screening; allows removal of polyps during the procedure

Stool-Based Tests

Fecal immunochemical test (FIT), guaiac-based test, or stool DNA tests detect hidden blood or DNA changes

Risk Assessment

Family history and genetic syndromes like Lynch syndrome require earlier and more frequent screening

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the stage, location, and patient health. A combination of therapies is often used.

Local Treatments

Surgery

Polypectomy

Removal of polyps during colonoscopy

Colectomy

Partial or total removal of the colon

Rectal Resection

Used for cancers located in the rectum

Radiation Therapy

  • • Often used for rectal cancer
  • • Can shrink tumors before surgery
  • • Combined with chemotherapy for advanced disease

Systemic Treatments

Chemotherapy

  • • Standard for stage III and IV colorectal cancer
  • • Often combined with surgery
  • • Reduces recurrence risk

Targeted Therapy

  • • For cancers with mutations (e.g., KRAS, BRAF)
  • • Includes anti-VEGF and anti-EGFR drugs

Immunotherapy

  • • For mismatch repair-deficient (dMMR) or microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) cancers
  • • Checkpoint inhibitors like pembrolizumab
